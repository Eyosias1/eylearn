# Metacognition & Calibration Research

> The evidence base behind [[confidence-rating]] and [[ai-grading]].

## Core concept

Metacognition — thinking about your own thinking — is one of the strongest predictors of academic performance. Students who accurately assess what they know and don't know study more efficiently. Most students are systematically overconfident.

The core problem: encoding-based study methods (re-reading, highlighting, summarizing, concept mapping) create an **illusion of knowing**. The material feels familiar, which students mistake for comprehension. But familiarity is not retrieval. When the exam arrives and there are no familiar cues from study material, recall fails.

## Key evidence

### Karpicke & Blunt (2011) — metacognitive predictions vs. actual performance

The same study that documented the testing effect also captured students' predictions about their own retention one week later. Results:

- Students predicted **repeated study** would yield the highest retention
- Students predicted **retrieval practice** would yield the worst retention
- Reality was exactly reversed: retrieval practice outperformed all other conditions by 50%
- Students also **overestimated** the effectiveness of studying once and concept mapping

This is a direct empirical demonstration of miscalibration in academic contexts. Students who rely on encoding-based methods are not just inefficient — they don't know they're inefficient.

**Citation:** Karpicke, J. D., & Blunt, J. R. (2011). Retrieval practice produces more learning than elaborative studying with concept mapping. *Science*, 331(6018), 772–775. DOI: 10.1126/science.1199327.
- [Full text — Science](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1199327)
- [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21252317/)

### Metcalfe (2017) — Learning from Errors

Reviews the broader literature on calibration and error-based learning. Students who are confidently wrong benefit more from corrective feedback than students who are unsure — a direct interaction between metacognitive confidence and learning outcome.

**Citation:** Metcalfe, J. (2017). Learning from errors. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 68, 465–489. DOI: 10.1146/annurev-psych-010416-044022.
- [Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-psych-010416-044022)
- [PDF — Columbia](https://www.columbia.edu/cu/psychology/metcalfe/PDFs/Learning%20from%20errorsAnnual%20ReviewMetcalfe2016.pdf)

### Additional evidence
- Dunning-Kruger effect — low-competence individuals systematically overestimate their performance
- Koriat & Bjork (2005) — illusions of competence arise specifically when study conditions resemble test conditions (e.g., reading notes that look like the test material)

## How it applies to eyLearn

- Confidence rating before each answer forces metacognitive engagement in real time
- [[ai-grading]] produces a calibration report at end of session showing how confidence correlated with actual performance
- Overconfident wrong answers (high confidence + wrong) are prioritized in [[spaced-repetition]] — these are the most dangerous misconceptions
- [[pre-test]] mode intentionally creates conditions where students are expected to be wrong, establishing accurate baseline calibration before studying
- [[blurting]] and [[feynman-technique]] both surface the illusion of knowing by requiring production rather than recognition

## Further reading

- [[active-recall]] — retrieval practice is the primary method for improving both performance and calibration
- [[hypercorrection-effect]] — being confidently wrong is actually useful: it produces stronger corrections than being unsure and wrong
