# Hypercorrection Effect Research

> The evidence base behind [[pre-test]].

## Core concept

When a learner answers a question with high confidence and gets it wrong, they are more likely to remember the correct answer after receiving feedback than when they answered with low confidence and got it wrong. Being confidently wrong creates a stronger correction signal.

This is counterintuitive — you'd expect high-confidence wrong answers to be harder to correct because the incorrect belief is more entrenched. In practice, the opposite is true: the surprise of the correction grabs attention, and learners rally attentional resources to encode the correct answer. The mechanism is partly surprise (violations of strong expectations demand cognitive engagement) and partly domain familiarity (high confidence correlates with knowing more about the topic in general, making the corrected answer easier to integrate).

## Key studies

### Butterfield & Metcalfe (2001) — Original documentation

Participants answered general world knowledge questions (e.g., "What poison did Socrates take at his execution?") and rated their confidence before receiving corrective feedback. High-confidence errors were more likely to be corrected on a delayed retest than low-confidence errors.

**Citation:** Butterfield, B., & Metcalfe, J. (2001). Errors committed with high confidence are hypercorrected. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 27(6), 1491–1494. PMID: 11713883.
- [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11713883/)

### Metcalfe (2017) — Learning from Errors (review)

Comprehensive review of the hypercorrection literature. Key conclusions:
- Errorful learning followed by corrective feedback is beneficial, not harmful
- The hypercorrection effect persists over a week for high-confidence errors
- High-confidence errors do not return at delay *if* a test is given after the corrective feedback; they do return if no test intervenes
- Error tolerance encourages active, exploratory, generative student engagement

**Citation:** Metcalfe, J. (2017). Learning from errors. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 68, 465–489. DOI: 10.1146/annurev-psych-010416-044022.
- [Annual Reviews](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-psych-010416-044022)
- [PDF — Columbia](https://www.columbia.edu/cu/psychology/metcalfe/PDFs/Learning%20from%20errorsAnnual%20ReviewMetcalfe2016.pdf)

## How the framework applies it

The framework explicitly uses the hypercorrection effect as the justification for **pretests prior to class** — one of six active recall application methods:

> "You are meant to get many of these wrong as you are trying to benefit from the hypercorrection effect. This effect refers to the tendency to remember and learn from corrections more effectively than if you were to answer the questions right initially."

Mechanism: students who attempt questions before studying are already primed to notice corrections. When they sit in lecture, they subconsciously undergo a correction process — identifying gaps in knowledge and picking up on subtle links between concepts. This keeps them more alert and engaged than students encountering material for the first time.

## How it applies to eyLearn

- [[pre-test]] mode quizzes users on material before they study it
- Users are expected to get most questions wrong — wrong answers are not penalized in pre-test mode
- Wrong answers from pre-test are flagged and surfaced first in the subsequent [[active-recall]] session
- [[confidence-rating]] in pre-test mode captures which wrong answers were high-confidence — these get the strongest priority boost in [[spaced-repetition]]
- Per Metcalfe (2017): a follow-up test after feedback is needed to lock in the correction — the second [[active-recall]] session serves this function
