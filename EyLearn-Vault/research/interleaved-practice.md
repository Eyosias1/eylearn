# Interleaved Practice Research

> The evidence base behind [[interleaved-practice]].

## Core concept

Mixing different topics or problem types during study (interleaving) produces better long-term retention than studying one topic at a time (blocked practice), even though blocked practice feels more productive in the moment.

Blocked practice — e.g., one hour on power rule, one hour on product rule — feels like mastery because the material is fresh. But it produces shallow encoding. Interleaving forces the brain to retrieve the right strategy from scratch each time, which is harder but creates stronger and more flexible memory.

Three reasons interleaving works:
1. **Spacing benefit** — naturally spaces out revisits to each topic, exploiting the forgetting curve
2. **Discrimination** — comparing different problem types in close proximity sharpens the ability to identify differences and similarities between concepts
3. **Real-world mimicry** — exams and professional work don't present topics in isolated blocks; interleaving prepares you for the actual conditions of recall

## Key studies

### Rohrer & Taylor (2007) — The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning

College students practiced volume problems in either blocked or interleaved order across two sessions one week apart. Both groups had the same problems and the same total time.

**Results:** Interleaving reduced practice scores but tripled test scores one week later (effect size d = 1.34). Students who used blocked practice performed significantly worse on the delayed test despite performing better during practice itself.

This is the foundational interleaved practice study — the 30%+ improvement figure cited in the framework comes from this line of research.

**Citation:** Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007). The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning. *Instructional Science*, 35, 481–498. DOI: 10.1007/s11251-007-9015-8
- [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-007-9015-8)
- [PDF — USF](http://uweb.cas.usf.edu/~drohrer/pdfs/Rohrer&Taylor2007IS.pdf)

### Rohrer, Dedrick & Stershic (2015) — Interleaved practice improves mathematics learning

126 seventh-grade students practiced math problems over **3 months** in a real classroom setting. Both groups received identical problems — the only difference was arrangement: blocked (grouped by strategy type) vs. interleaved (mixed). Students then took unannounced tests at two timepoints.

**Results:**
- Immediate test: interleaved outperformed blocked, **d = 0.42**
- 30-day delayed test: interleaved outperformed blocked, **d = 0.79**

The effect *grew* with delay — interleaving's advantage compounds over time. The mechanism: interleaved students had to independently select the right problem-solving strategy each time, with no cue about which lesson it came from. This mirrors real exam and real-world conditions.

**Citation:** Rohrer, D., Dedrick, R. F., & Stershic, S. (2015). Interleaved practice improves mathematics learning. *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 107(3), 900–908. DOI: 10.1037/edu0000001
- [PDF — USF](http://uweb.cas.usf.edu/~drohrer/pdfs/Rohrer_et_al_2015JEdPsych.pdf)
- [ERIC](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED557355.pdf)

## Application methods (from the framework)

### 1. Switching subjects/topics
Break a study day into 45-minute blocks (long enough for flow state, short enough to stay focused). Rather than completing all blocks for one subject before moving to the next, rotate subjects across the day. This creates active engagement with each topic throughout the entire day and builds cross-topic linkages.

### 2. Switching modes
Four modes of learning: pretests, lectures, after-class reviews, homeworks. Intuitive approach is to complete all four for one subject in one day. But this is too easy — material is fresh, so cognitive effort is low, and long-term encoding is weak.

Better approach: interleave modes across days. Example: pretest for subject A on Monday, review subject B notes after class on Tuesday, do subject A homework on Wednesday. Forces stronger retrieval effort and spaces out revisits to each subject.

### 3. Ideal time for switching
- Lower bound: ~25 minutes (Pomodoro minimum; shorter intervals prevent entering flow state)
- Upper bound: ~90 minutes (aligned with ultradian rhythms observed in sleep cycles, hormone release, and brain activity — all linked to memory consolidation)

Experiment within this range to find your personal optimal switching interval.
