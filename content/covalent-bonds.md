# Covalent Bonds

A covalent bond forms when two atoms **share electrons** to achieve a stable electron configuration.

## Formation

Atoms share electrons to fill their valence shells (octet rule):

$$\text{H} \cdot + \cdot \text{H} \rightarrow \text{H:H} \quad (H_2)$$

## Bond Types

| Type | Shared Pairs | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Single bond | 1 | $H_2$, $CH_4$ |
| Double bond | 2 | $O_2$, $CO_2$ |
| Triple bond | 3 | $N_2$, $C_2H_2$ |

More shared pairs → **shorter**, **stronger** bond.

## Polar vs Non-Polar

**Non-polar** — equal sharing (same electronegativity):
$$\text{Cl—Cl, H—H}$$

**Polar** — unequal sharing creates a dipole $\delta^+ \cdots \delta^-$:
$$\delta^+ H \text{—} Cl\ \delta^-$$

Electronegativity difference > 0.4 → polar covalent.

## Lewis Structures

Dots represent valence electrons. Shared pairs become bonds:

$$\text{H:O:H} \Rightarrow H_2O \text{ (two bonds + two lone pairs on O)}$$

## Bond Energy

Energy required to break 1 mol of a bond in the gas phase:

| Bond | Energy (kJ/mol) |
|---|---|
| C—C | 347 |
| C=C | 614 |
| C≡C | 839 |
| O—H | 463 |

## Organic Applications

Covalent bonds are the backbone of organic molecules. See [[Organic Chemistry Esters|Esters]] for how single and double bonds form functional groups.
